01 / Foundation

The five phases of writing an academic essay

An academic essay is not one big leap — it is five phases. Sek II writing adds research and an explicit thesis-development step on top of the four phases you learned in Sek I.

①
Research
Read the question. Read around it. Note quotations, statistics, cases, authors.
②
Plan

③

④

⑤
Submit
Final proofread. Reference list. Word count. Submission cover note.

01

Session 1 · Research & Plan

For an academic essay you cannot just brainstorm and write. You first research the question, then form a clear thesis, then plan your argument map. This is the slow, careful work that makes a good essay possible.

⏱ 90 minutes total
1.1 Decode the question ⏱ 10'

Read your essay question slowly. Most Sek II tasks bury at least four things inside one sentence. Identify them before you do anything else:

① Operator(s)What is the verb of the task? (discuss, comment on, analyze, evaluate, assess…)
See §8 of the Berlin handout for the official Sek II operator definitions and what each one demands.
② Topic / domainWhich field is this in? Politics, ethics, literature, science, society, economics?
Name the field — it tells you what kind of evidence will count.
③ Key conceptsWhich terms must you define before you can argue?
Underline every term that more than one reasonable person could understand differently.
④ Hidden assumptionsWhat does the question take for granted? What could you challenge?
Good answers often start by questioning a premise the question quietly assumes.
Tip · operators (§8 of the Berlin handout) The operator tells you the cognitive level required. Discuss means: arguments for AND against + well-founded conclusion. Comment on means: reasoned personal position with evidence. Evaluate / assess means: well-founded judgement on quality, success, or impact.
1.2 Research the question ⏱ 35'

Sek II essays must show that you have read around the question. You do not need to read everything; you need to read strategically.

For each key concept and each side of the argument, find:

  • At least one primary source (an author's own words, a statute, a literary text, a peer-reviewed paper, an original document). For philosophy: e.g. the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy will point you to the canonical texts. For history/politics: original speeches, treaties, press releases. For literature: the work itself.
  • At least one good reference work (encyclopaedia, handbook, scholarly introduction).
  • At least one current example (a case, a piece of recent legislation, a news story, a contemporary debate).
Quoting and referencing (§7.6 of the handout) Always record exact source, exact page, exact wording in your notes. You can decide later whether to quote directly or paraphrase, but you cannot reconstruct what you didn't record. Use a citation system (Chicago humanities, MLA, or APA) and stick to one.
Originality Never paste a passage from a source into your draft. Always read, then close the source, then write the idea in your own words from your notes. This is the single most important habit for keeping your essay honest.
1.3 Build your thesis statement ⏱ 15'

A thesis is a one-sentence answer to the essay question that you will defend. A good thesis is specific, arguable, and gives the shape of your argument.

Weak (description)States a fact or restates the question.
"Social media has effects on young people, both positive and negative."
Stronger (claim)Takes a position.
"Social media does more harm than good to teenagers' mental health."
Best (specific + arguable + roadmap)Position + key reasons + nuance.
"The dominant narrative that social media universally harms teenagers conceals a more uneven picture: while heavy use of image-based platforms is reliably linked to lower self-esteem in adolescent girls, moderate, community-based use can support identity formation and access to support — so the policy answer is not abstinence but design reform."
The thesis test Could a reasonable, well-read person disagree with your thesis? If not, it's too obvious. Sharpen it.
1.4 Map your argument (graphic organizer) ⏱ 20'

Don't start drafting yet. First build a map of the case you will make. Choose the organizer that fits:

TableTwo or three columns. Best for pro/contra, before/after, position/counter-position.
| Claim | Evidence | Counter-argument | Response |
Tree diagramTop: thesis. Branches: main arguments. Sub-branches: evidence + objections. Best for complex chains of reasoning.
Thesis → Arg 1 → (ev, obj, response) · Arg 2 → … · Arg 3 → …
Concept mapConcepts in boxes, relations on arrows. Best when key terms interact in complex ways.
concept A →(causes)→ concept B →(undermines)→ concept C
The "so what?" check For every argument on your map, ask: so what? Why does this matter for the thesis? If you cannot answer in one sentence, the argument doesn't belong in this essay.
1.5 End of Session 1 — save your plan ⏱ 10'

Before you stop: read your thesis and argument map back to yourself. Do they hang together? Have you actually answered the question? Note any gaps now, before you start drafting.

Back up your work Everything in this pathway is saved in your browser only. Copy your notes, thesis and argument map into a cloud document or e-mail them to yourself.
02

Session 2 · Draft your essay

You now have a thesis, evidence and a structure. Today you turn them into prose — following the genre template you chose. Don't aim for perfection; aim for a complete draft you can revise next session.

2.2 Phrase bank: linking words & academic register ⏱ 10'

Sek II essays use connectives consciously. Browse and copy what you need; you can return here any time during drafting.

Adding an argument
  • Moreover, …
  • Furthermore, …
  • In addition, …
  • What is more, …
  • Beyond this, …
Drawing contrasts
  • However, …
  • By contrast, …
  • On the other hand, …
  • Whereas X holds Y, …
  • Nevertheless, …
Conceding & refuting
  • It is true that …, but …
  • Granted that …, it does not follow that …
  • Critics object that … Yet …
  • A natural objection is …
  • This objection underestimates …
Cause & consequence
  • Consequently, …
  • It follows that …
  • This entails that …
  • As a result, …
  • For this reason, …
Citing & referring
  • As [Author] ([year]) argues, …
  • According to …, …
  • This view is defended by …
  • In a recent study, … shows that …
  • The Stanford Encyclopedia notes that …
Hedging (avoiding overclaim)
  • This suggests that …
  • It seems plausible that …
  • There is reason to think that …
  • One could argue …
  • To some extent, …
Concluding
  • In sum, …
  • To conclude, …
  • On balance, …
  • The upshot is that …
  • What follows from all this is …
Avoid these (too informal)
  • ✗ a lot of → many / much / a great deal of
  • ✗ stuff / things → factors / aspects / considerations
  • ✗ I think → I argue / I contend
  • ✗ kinda / sorta → in some sense / to some degree
  • ✗ contractions (don't, isn't) → do not, is not

Click any phrase above to copy it.

2.3 Write your first full draft ⏱ 60'

Now write. Keep your thesis, argument map and template open. Aim for a complete first draft, paragraph by paragraph. Don't stop to polish — push through to the end.

  • One paragraph = one idea. Begin every paragraph with a topic sentence.
  • Evidence comes second, analysis comes third. Never let a quotation or fact stand alone — always explain what it shows.
  • Address counter-arguments early. A strong essay anticipates objections rather than ignoring them.
  • Cite as you go. Drop in (Author, year, p. X) markers while drafting; you can format properly later.
0 words
Tip · pacing in 60 minutes A useful pace for an ~1,800-word essay: 5 min introduction · 10 min per body paragraph (assume 4–5) · 5 min conclusion. If you fall behind, write tighter, not faster.
Don't let the AI write You can use AI for feedback in Session 3, never to draft sentences or paragraphs. The essay has to be in your own voice and your own words — that is what the Berlin Rahmenlehrplan and academic integrity expect.
2.4 End of Session 2 — let the draft rest ⏱ 10'

Read your draft once, slowly. Don't fix anything yet. Distance is what makes revision possible. Save your draft, back it up, and come back to it after at least one full night's sleep.

03

Session 3 · Revise & submit

Today you self-check your draft against the Berlin criteria, then ask a privacy-safe AI tool for structured feedback, then implement the feedback and finalise.

⏱ 90 minutes total
3.1 Self-check against the genre checklist ⏱ 15'

Read your draft from yesterday. Tick what you have already done well. Don't tick anything you're unsure about — leave it empty for now. Anything still unticked after this round goes on your revision to-do list.

Pick a level and a text type above to see your checklist.
Tip · proofreading (§7.7 of the handout) Read your text aloud — slowly. Read it backwards (last sentence up) to catch spelling mistakes. Cover the lines below the one you are reading. Check your "favourite mistakes" list.
3.2 Ask the AI for structured feedback (not rewriting) ⏱ 15'

Open your school's privacy-safe AI tool (e.g. fobizz Tools, SchulKI, telli) and use the prompt below. The prompt is built around the same checklist criteria you used above — so the AI will check the four areas you need: content & argument, structure, vocabulary & register, grammar & mechanics.

Pick your text type and level above to see the matching AI prompt.
Critical · how to use AI legitimately The AI must not rewrite your text. The prompt below instructs the model to give diagnostic feedback only: where to improve, why, and what to try — without producing replacement sentences. This is the only kind of AI use that keeps the writing yours.
Privacy Use only a privacy-safe AI tool. Don't paste names, addresses or anything personally identifying. Replace with placeholders like [my school].
What you get back Feedback in four diagnostic sections. Don't accept everything blindly. The AI can be wrong — especially about subject-matter content. You decide what to change.
3.3 Implement the feedback ⏱ 40'

Work through the feedback systematically. Tackle big issues first (argument, structure, missing counter-arguments), then language (vocabulary, register, grammar). After each change, re-read the surrounding paragraph to make sure it still flows.

0 words
Tip · revise in passes, not all at once Pass 1 = content & argument. Pass 2 = structure & paragraph order. Pass 3 = vocabulary & register. Pass 4 = grammar & mechanics. Trying to do everything at once costs you accuracy.
3.4 References, word count & formatting ⏱ 15'

Final formal steps before submission:

  1. Format every in-text citation consistently (one system only — Chicago, MLA or APA).
  2. Compile your reference list at the end of the essay. Alphabetical order.
  3. Run a word count. Check it against the limit your task sets.
  4. Add a title page if required: title, your name, course/class, date.
  5. Write a short process reflection for yourself (see template below) — it helps you learn from this essay and is useful evidence if anyone ever asks how you worked.
Keep your working files (1) Your final essay. (2) Your reference list. (3) A short note on how you used AI. (4) Your drafts & brainstorm notes. These document your process — useful for self-review, for teacher feedback, and as proof of originality if anyone ever asks.
3.5 Final proof & submit ⏱ 5'

One last read-through. If something still feels off, fix it now, not after submitting.
